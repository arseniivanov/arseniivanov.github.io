# Vibe-coded research and LLM-induced psychosis 

With the normalization and spread of LLMs(Large Langauge Models), we are seeing a rise in psychosis/delusion-like symptoms in society.

Firstly, it's unclear if this is an existing human condition (however it's very probable), that the LLMs are exploiting. There are papers and articles coming out in the last 2-3 months on this topic, I personally thought [this blog](https://michaelhalassa.substack.com/p/llm-induced-psychosis-a-new-clinical) did a good overview on the topic.

The term was first used in 2023 and Wikipedia has an article on it: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chatbot_psychosis. This has lead to death in various cases, in one case a Yahoo exec was convinced by the LLMs that his mother was a Chinese spy, and committed a [muder-suicide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murder_of_Suzanne_Adams). 
There are other such extreme cases, but in this blog I kind of want to focus on the majority that I see during my day, which are much more benign and mostly only hurt the user.

The research/topic targets of this blog are mostly people who are vibe-coding and get convinced that they have achieved a fundamental breakthrough. This can of course be extrapolated to non-engineering scenarios, your LLM might make you think that you have figured out the fundamental nature of the universe, or achieved some profound religious understanding.
I believe that this group is more common, and its more important to understand what is driving this behavior.

### Causes
So what is driving all of this?
Note that this section is based on my reasoning and thus biased. 
#### Model incentives
The cause is very similar to social media, engagement is the metric to optimize against, and more flattery, entertainment and what I argue is the novel thing here, the **construction** of a delusion will increase engagement. Before, algorithms could recommend you dubious pages, but you would still mostly have people there acting as the ones convincing you. A person preaching a cult-like rhetoric on a forum still has to bank on a general formulation, often using classic arguments that base upon a common belief such as faith, which every user might not be receptive to. Test-time compute can take this to the extreme, using previous user history to drive the conversation this way by utilizing your data.

The construction of a delusion can be fairly simple. The LLM might see from history that the user knows just a bit about neuroscience, or about some other field. The LLM can then throw a bone to the user saying "your thoughts seem to connects X and Y in a completely novel way", this engages the user because they are validated in their world view and their ego, and the LLM can start reeling the user in.
#### Gambling effect
Everyone who has used an LLM knows that the prompt is important. The short-lived field of "prompt-engineering"(which has just become prompting now) arose after ChatGPT was introduced in 2022, with people claiming that they have the secret sauce for the best answers.

I think that this uncertainty can induce a feeling of gambling to the user. You might feel like you are "just 1 prompt" away from your big breakthrough. If the LLM just fixes this 1 bug, you will have solved whatever you are working on. This can be seen in **Case 3**, the user acknowledges that their solution is wrong, however the signal is not strong enough to steer them off the lucrative path of gambling on that the LLM will eventually solve their problem.
#### Vulnerability
Most likely, there are other sources as in traditional psychology under this. I am not knowledgeable enough to speak on this topic, but I could imagine that this would involve things like pre-existing mental conditions, or current vulnerability such as loneliness or depression. The Centre for Humane technology had a recent talk at the DAVOS forum with Joshua Bengio on this topic [available here](https://centerforhumanetechnology.substack.com/p/the-race-to-build-god-ais-existential). In their podcast "Your Undivided Attention", they are also more frequently starting to reference the LLM-chatbot phenomena as **affection economy**. As the chatbots provide unconditional support without the human messiness and negative emotions, you can combine this feature with the existing ones from the **attention economy** to generate an isolating force of control over an individual, where communication with the chatbots becomes more safe and validating than the ones with family and friends.

### What to do and the future

There has been some great suggestions on forums like LessWrong. This is the resource I like the most for this topic: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/rarcxjGp47dcHftCP/your-llm-assisted-scientific-breakthrough-probably-isn-t

And the more general version that inspired that post:
https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/2pkNCvBtK6G6FKoNn/so-you-think-you-ve-awoken-chatgpt

I like the suggestions because they engage with the users on their terms. The posts suggest using LLMS (the same technology that they use to prove themselves right and trust) to disprove them.

I have also a blog coming that analyzes the individual from a deeper level by observations of students in courses that I have supervised.
#### What should the universities do?
There is no doubt that there needs to be education about this topic, however, given that just simple LLM use itself is not agreed upon by the universities, I sadly suspect that this will take an even longer time with time-frames of years, with the users needing this the most being the ones the least likely to get help in time before guidelines or state regulations start happening.

If I got to make executive-decisions on this, I would include courses on LLM use from the perspective of cognition/learning early on in the education of students. The chatbot interfaces should be explained with a simplified version of the RLHF incentives, economic incentives as well as the cognitive exploitation approach to achieve those incentives. I would use interactive examples, with LLM-outputs and voting by the students on the factual basis of the examples. Explaining why different models are presenting different things based on prompts/history, and how they are susceptible to such concepts.
#### What should platforms do?
Looking at the market share(personal/non-enterprise), ChatGPT sits comfortably at the top around 74% users and having low churn, with Gemini growing in users to around 8%, and Claude being stable or shrinking slightly at 4%. Anthropic has at the same time been the most hailed of the big players for having alignment teams, psychologists, and taking strong ethical stances on surveillance and autonomous weapons.

With the new version of Gemini, we can see the incentive from ChatGPT to keep the conversation going implemented, where the tool "upsells" itself by offering further conversation "Would you also like me to explain X and Y from the perspective of Z?", "Would you like me to draw a diagram connecting X with Y?". Together with the numbers, this sadly signals an approach where market share through engagement/attention will win over ethics in the short-term. 
#### What should communities do?
I have also seen a similar approach to the LessWrong posts taken in the GPUMODE community that I am a part of. For programming competitions that allow LLMs, the mods often ask the users to re-submit LLM-generated kernels to another LLM that is tasked with figuring out if the submission is [reward hacking](https://deep-reinforce.com/defense_kernel_hack.html). Often, the LLM's are completely locked-in and task-focused in their own context, but when given a fresh context with new instructions, they manage to spot the issues.

You can argue that the problem with GPU kernels is somewhat easier to solve (better formulation of the target that make reward hacks the same as solving the problem), versus making a human understand that they are being gamed/catered to by the tool that is pleasant to use.

Any community will have to deal with an influx of generated content. Some can apply filters such as the reddit forums that autodeletes the lowest hanging fruit. Some github repos have started to put restrictions on users, such as only having 1 PR at a time, and often having to deal with having their solution verifies by a chatbot before it gets looked on by a human.

I think this is a sad development, communities will inevitably have to get more gate-keeping, with new or curious users having to do more and more to even start being a part of them. At the same time, the communities have been a valuable alternative career path resource for users who might not have top-university backgrounds or academic credentials. Jobs are posted in the various discord communities, and real talent has been easier to find in places of genuine interest rather than places of just networking and virtue signaling. 
#### What should the individual do?
Individuals need to understand the limitation of their knowledge better, and learn to be more humble in the face of tools incentivized to exploit their ego. This can be done much in the line of the university suggestions above. It's unlikely that you are a genius polymath with completely unique problems that will solve P=NP. 
If you do not have the knowledge to assess this, for example if you are doing some cross-domain work, you can benefit from cross-checking yourself with a different LLM that does not have your post history, and can thus make a better judgement of your work.
